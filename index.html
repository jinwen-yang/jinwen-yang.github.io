Jinwen Yang

I am a final-year PhD student in the Department of Statistics at University of Chicago, advised by Prof. Haihao Lu. Previously, I obtained my B.S. in Mathematics and Applied Mathematics from Fudan University.

My research focuses on developing computational and mathematical methods for large-scale optimization in operations research, data science, and machine learning. Specifically, I am interested in: (i) designing algorithms that are well-suited to modern computational infrastructure, such as GPUs; and (ii) developing accessible, open-source software that facilitates seamless GPU integration in practical applications. Portions of my work have been adopted and integrated into several leading technology companies and commercial solvers, including Gurobi, NVIDIA, Cardinal Optimizer (COPT), FICO Xpress, Artelys Knitro, and others.

Publications

cuPDLP. jl: A GPU implementation of restarted primal-dual hybrid gradient for linear programming in Julia

H. Lu and J. Yang

To appear in Operations Research.

preprint software

cuPDLP.jl is the first open-source GPU-based solver for linear programming using restarted PDHG, offering a new horse in the race alongside commercial solvers like Gurobi. It shows that first-order methods on GPUs can scale efficiently and solve large LPs with competitive performance.

A Practical and Optimal First-Order Method for Large-Scale Convex Quadratic Programming

H. Lu and J. Yang

To appear in Mathematical Programming.

preprint paper software

We propose a restarted and accelerated first-order method for solving large-scale convex quadratic programs, which achieves optimal linear convergence and is fully factorization-free. Backed by the theory, the open-source solver PDQP.jl delivers strong performance on both CPU and GPU across standard and large-scale QP benchmarks.

On the geometry and refined rate of primal–dual hybrid gradient for linear programming

H. Lu and J. Yang

Mathematical Programming, 212, 349–387 (2025).

preprint paper

We provide the refined complexity analysis of the primal-dual hybrid gradient (PDHG) method for linear programming, revealing a two-stage convergence behavior driven by local geometry rather than the loose global Hoffman constant. Our results explain why PDHG scales efficiently in practice, even for degenerate LPs.

A  J-symmetric quasi-newton method for minimax problems

A. Asl, H. Lu and J. Yang

Mathematical Programming, 204, 207–254 (2024).

preprint paper

We propose a novel quasi-Newton method tailored for minimax optimization by leveraging the J-symmetric structure of the problem's second-order derivatives. Our method generalizes the PSB update, ensures efficient and stable rank-2 updates, and achieves both local superlinear and global convergence.

An Overview of GPU-based First-Order Methods for Linear Programming and Extensions

H. Lu and J. Yang

preprint

We overview the emerging field of GPU-based first-order methods for linear programming, with a focus on the design, theory, and performance of cuPDLP. This work positions GPU-accelerated solvers as a scalable, competitive alternative to traditional CPU-based methods, opening new possibilities for large-scale optimization based on GPUs.

cuPDLPx: A Further Enhanced GPU-Based First-Order Solver for Linear Programming

H. Lu, Z. Peng and J. Yang

preprint software

cuPDLPx is a new GPU-based linear programming solver incorporating recent algorithmic advances like the restarted Halpern PDHG method and a PID-controlled primal weight update. These innovations, specifically tailored for GPU architectures, result in a 2x to 6x speedup over cuPDLP on standard LP benchmark datasets. The improvements are most pronounced in high-accuracy settings and on harder problem instances.

New Understandings and Computation on Augmented Lagrangian Methods for Low-Rank Semidefinite Programming

L. Ding, H. Lu and J. Yang

preprint

We establish new theoretical foundations for augmented Lagrangian methods (ALM) in low-rank semidefinite programming by showing that ALM subproblems inherit key structural properties from the original SDP and can be solved globally via gradient descent. Building on these insights, we design ALORA, a GPU-accelerated, rank-adaptive solver that scales to SDPs with tens of millions of variables.

MPAX: Mathematical Programming in JAX

H. Lu, Z. Peng and J. Yang

preprint software

MPAX is a JAX-based mathematical programming solver optimized for machine learning workflows, offering GPU-accelerated, batchable, and differentiable first-order methods. It features such as warm start, auto-diff, and device parallelism.

Restarted Halpern PDHG for Linear Programming

H. Lu and J. Yang

preprint

We propose restarted Halpern PDHG, a matrix-free primal-dual algorithm for linear programming, which achieves accelerated convergence for both feasible and infeasible LPs. Our GPU-based solver built on the proposed algorithm outperforms existing matrix-free methods like cuPDLP, offering practical speedups and refined theoretical guarantees including two-stage acceleration and improved infeasibility detection.

PDOT: A practical primal-dual algorithm and a GPU-based solver for optimal transport

H. Lu and J. Yang

preprint software

We develop PDOT, a first-order method for large-scale discrete optimal transport that exploits problem structure for efficiency. It offers provable local linear convergence with polynomial complexity, and the open-source PDOT.jl delivers order-of-magnitude speedups over Gurobi while achieving much higher accuracy than Sinkhorn on benchmarks.

cuPDLP-C: A strengthened implementation of cuPDLP for linear programming by C language

H. Lu, J. Yang, H. Hu, Q. Huangfu, J. Liu, T. Liu, Y. Ye, C. Zhang and D. Ge

preprint software

cuPDLP-C brings GPU-accelerated first-order methods for linear programming to new heights by reimplementing cuPDLP in C, achieving major speedups over the Julia version and demonstrating competitiveness, even outperforming commercial solvers, on massive LP benchmarks.

On a Unified and Simplified Proof for the Ergodic Convergence Rates of PPM, PDHG and ADMM

H. Lu and J. Yang

preprint

Motivated by the desire for a teachable explanation in a graduate course, we develop a unified perspective showing that PPM, PDHG, and ADMM are all instances of the same generic algorithm under a norm change, clarifying intuition and enabling a four-line proof of their convergence rates.
